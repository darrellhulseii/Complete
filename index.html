Reference — Warren Buffett on Business & Marketing

Berkshire Hathaway
On what makes a business worth owning
A truly great business must have an enduring "moat" that protects excellent returns on invested capital
His single clearest statement of the moat concept — a durable edge (brand, cost structure, licensing, reputation) that competitors can't easily cross. Ask what your version of a moat is across all three dealer channels: is it your install quality, your response time, your local reputation?
Berkshire Hathaway 2007 Shareholder Letter (PDF) →
On running a business day to day — internal memo to Berkshire's managers, Sept 2001
Widen the moat, build enduring competitive advantage, delight your customers, and relentlessly fight costs
Written days after 9/11, telling every Berkshire manager what not to change despite the crisis. Directly applicable as a monthly checklist for your operation: are you strengthening your edge, keeping customers genuinely happy, and cutting waste — regardless of what's happening in the wider market that month?
Berkshire Hathaway — Message to Managers and Associates (official memo) →
On evaluating a business's competitive strength — 1995 Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting
trying to find a business with a wide and long-lasting moat around it (protecting "a terrific economic castle with an honest lord in charge")
From the Q&A format Buffett used for decades at Berkshire's annual meetings — unscripted, direct answers to shareholder questions. The "honest lord in charge" phrase matters as much as the moat itself: he ties competitive advantage directly to trustworthy management, which maps onto how you'd want each of your ADT/Frontier/AT&T contracts represented to customers.
